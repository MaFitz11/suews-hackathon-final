SUEWS Community Hackathon / UDA-city

Heat adaptation is most powerful when it is targeted.

I modelled a synthetic hot-humid city under current climate, a hotter +2.5 C future, and a constrained adaptation future. A feasible package reduces citywide heat-risk pressure by 15.4%, guarantees at least 5% improvement in every neighbourhood, and gives larger gains to the places with the greatest vulnerability.

The research question

Where is heat most dangerous to people in UDA-city, and how much can a realistic adaptation package reduce that danger? The answer is not simply the hottest neighbourhood. Risk emerges where heat, exposure, and vulnerability overlap.

A feasible adaptation package

The adaptation scenario is constrained on purpose. It does not assume unlimited redesign. Instead, it uses a citywide budget and allocates it where it has the largest justice benefit.

Land

Convert up to 20% of total paved and bare soil area into evergreen trees, grass, and water.

Cooling

Increase citywide cooling access by 50% of current access, targeted toward vulnerability.

Transport heat

Assume 15% vehicle heat reduction as a policy lever. This dataset has QF off, so it is not active in the SUEWS run.

Fairness

Every neighbourhood must improve by at least 5%; extra benefit goes to the highest-risk hotspots.

Why allocation matters

Spreading resources evenly would not be fair. Hotspot neighbourhoods have lower cooling access, more outdoor work, higher deprivation, and dense exposure. The package therefore uses a minimum guarantee for everyone, then directs extra cooling resources to the places where heat risk is socially concentrated.

Justice result

The targeted scenario meets the fairness rule: every neighbourhood reaches at least 5% reduction in heat-risk pressure, with the largest gains in the currently most vulnerable hotspots.

What the result means

The hotter future sharply increases dangerous heat hours across UDA-city. Greening helps, but it does not solve the problem alone. The strongest adaptation story combines physical cooling with social protection: more shade and evapotranspiration, better access to cooling, and priority for neighbourhoods with low cooling access, outdoor work, deprivation, and child exposure.

The scenario is feasible because it respects limits: only part of paved/bare land can change, cooling access is budgeted citywide, and vehicle heat is acknowledged as a policy lever without pretending it is active in the current SUEWS configuration.
